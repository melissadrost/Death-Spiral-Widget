🌀 The Death Spiral – Peloton FY2025 10-K Case Study

Cost Allocation Economics | Real Data from PTON 10-K (FY ended June 30, 2025)
What is the Death Spiral? When volume falls, fixed capacity costs must be spread over fewer units — raising unit cost. Higher unit costs can trigger further volume reductions, raising costs again… and so on. Peloton's post-COVID trajectory is a textbook demand-driven death spiral: peak subscribers in FY2021 → rising per-unit costs → price sensitivity → subscriber defection → even higher per-unit costs.

📉 The Spiral Timeline: From Peak to Recovery Attempt
FY2021 (Peak)
COVID boom: ~2.33M Connected Fitness Subscribers. Revenue surges. Fixed costs spread over maximum volume → lowest per-unit overhead. Stock hits ~$170.
FY2022 (Inflection)
Post-COVID demand collapse. Inventory glut ($1.4B+). Tread+ recall. Fixed costs now spread over fewer units → unit costs spike. Net loss: $(2.8B). First restructuring announced.
FY2023 (Deep Spiral)
Hardware revenue falls to $1.13B. Hardware gross margin: negative ($(198.6M)). Fixed costs crushing each unit. Net loss: $(1.26B). Churn rises to 1.3%.
FY2024 (Continued Pressure)
Hardware revenue: $991.7M (↓12%). Hardware gross margin improves to just 4.9%. Subscribers fall to 2.98M. Churn: 1.4%. Net loss: $(552M).
FY2025 (Stabilization)
Hardware revenue: $817M (↓17.6%). But hardware gross margin improves to 13.6% — cost restructuring working. Subscribers: 2.80M. Churn: 1.6%. Net loss narrows to $(119M). Adj. EBITDA: $404M. Free cash flow: $324M.
🎓 Teaching Point: Peloton's spiral was demand-driven. The fixed costs of studios, instructors, content production, and platform infrastructure (~$516M subscription COGS + $705M hardware COGS = $1.22B total COGS in FY25) don't disappear when subscribers leave. Each departing subscriber forces the remaining ones to absorb more fixed cost per unit — exactly the mechanism described in your slides.

Hardware Death Spiral: Peloton Connected Fitness Products

Peloton's hardware segment shows the supply-driven spiral most clearly. As unit volumes fell post-COVID, fixed manufacturing overhead, warehousing, and logistics costs were spread over fewer bikes and treads — driving unit costs above selling prices.

🎓 Key Insight for Peloton: If Peloton uses the actual rate (dividing fixed costs by current low subscriber count), each subscriber appears very expensive — which could push management to raise prices, accelerating churn. The practical capacity rate keeps the per-subscriber cost stable, and explicitly reports the cost of unused capacity as a separate line item — making the excess capacity problem visible and manageable rather than hidden in inflated product costs.

🛡️ Escaping the Spiral — What Peloton Did (and Still Needs to Do)

Peloton's FY25 10-K shows a company that has partially escaped the spiral through aggressive cost restructuring. Here's the playbook — and what the textbook says about each approach.

Strategy 1: Cut Fixed Costs (Restructuring)

📖 Theory (Your Slides)

Practical capacity rates can be a short-term solution. If you can reduce the fixed cost base itself, the spiral stops. Cutting excess capacity eliminates the overhead that was being spread over too few units.

🏢 Peloton's Actions (FY22–FY25)

  • 3 restructuring plans (2022, 2024, 2025)
  • Headcount: ~8,600 → ~2,656 employees
  • Retail showrooms closed (exit strategy)
  • Closed Peloton Output Park manufacturing
  • OpEx cut 25% YoY in FY25
  • $200M+ run-rate savings from 2022/2024 plans
  • $100M+ targeted from 2025 plan
Strategy 2: Grow Volume to Absorb Fixed Costs

📖 Theory (Clay Sprays Example)

Adding volume (like acquiring Coronas) lowers average fixed cost per unit. BUT: if the new volume has zero contribution margin, total profitability doesn't improve — you just shift costs around.

🏢 Peloton's Attempts

  • B2B / Peloton for Business (hotels, gyms)
  • Rental program ($45.8M revenue FY25)
  • App subscriptions (552K paid app subs)
  • Strength+ standalone app (new FY25)
  • Used Equipment Activation Fee (FY25)
  • Peloton Repowered resale marketplace (Jun 2025)
  • ⚠️ App subs fell from 621K → 552K in FY25
Strategy 3: Use Practical Capacity Rates to Avoid Spiral Pricing

📖 Theory

Allocate overhead based on practical capacity, not actual volume. This keeps per-unit costs stable even as volume falls, preventing the cost-driven price increases that accelerate defections.

🏢 Peloton's Approach

Peloton held its All-Access price at $44/month throughout the spiral — a critical decision. Rather than raising prices to recover fixed costs (which would accelerate churn), they absorbed the losses and cut costs instead. The 10-K notes 99% of connected fitness subscribers are on month-to-month plans — any price increase would immediately test churn sensitivity.

🎓 Bottom Line: Peloton has slowed the spiral through aggressive cost-cutting, but hasn't fully escaped it. The company is in a race: can it grow subscribers (or find new revenue streams) fast enough to re-spread its fixed costs before the next round of cost-cutting erodes the product quality that keeps subscribers loyal? This is the fundamental tension the death spiral creates — and why it's so dangerous.
